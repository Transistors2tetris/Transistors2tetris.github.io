Overview

A computer is an electronic device that processes data according to a set of instructions, known as programs. It can perform a wide range of tasks, from simple arithmetic operations to complex simulations and data analysis. The fundamental components of a computer include hardware and software. Hardware refers to the physical components such as the central processing unit (CPU), memory, and input/output devices. Software comprises the programs and operating systems that control the hardware and enable the computer to perform specific tasks. Computers operate on binary logic, using a series of electrical signals represented as ones (1) and zeros (0). This binary system is the foundation of all computing processes, enabling the execution of complex algorithms and operations that power modern technology.The evolution of computers can be traced back to ancient mechanical devices, progressing through various technological advancements that have shaped modern computing.

MECHANICAL COMPUTERS

One of the earliest forms of mechanical computation was the abacus, used for arithmetic calculations. In the 17th century, Blaise Pascal developed the Pascaline, a mechanical calculator capable of performing addition and subtraction. Later, in the 19th century, Charles Babbage designed the Analytical Engine, a conceptual mechanical computer that could be programmed using punched cards. Although never fully built, the Analytical Engine laid the groundwork for modern computer design.

RELAY COMPUTERS

The early 20th century saw the advent of electromechanical computers using relays, which are switches operated by electrical currents. One notable example is the Zuse Z3, developed by Konrad Zuse in 1941. The Z3 was the world's first programmable computer, using relays to perform arithmetic operations and data storage. Constructed using 2000 relays, it featured a 22-bit word length and operated at a clock frequency of approximately 5–10 Hz. Program code and data for the Z3 were stored on punched film. This pioneering machine was completed in Berlin in 1941.

Wake word detection

Within the ALICE system, a vital component known as the wake word (“ALICE”,”Hey ALICE”) serves as the trigger for initiating a conversation with the voice assistant. This wake word acts as a specific term or phrase that prompts the system to become active and establish communication with the user. To achieve this functionality, a dedicated process within ALICE continuously monitors and analyzes an ongoing stream of audio input, specifically focusing on detecting the occurrence of the predefined wake word. The whole process can be summarized as follows.

The wake word detector is initialized and loaded with the "alice.pmdl" model that was trained on a Docker training interface. It then starts capturing audio from the chosen audio input source (microphone).The wake word detector performs feature extraction on the audio frames to obtain acoustic features, in our case, we use a feature known as Mel Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCCs).The Deep Neural Network (DNN) feeds the MFCC features forward through its layers, performing calculations and activations in comparison to the fed-trained PMDL file. The output of the DNN is a probability score (on a scale of 0 to 1) that indicates the likelihood of the wake word "ALICE" being present in the current audio frame

SPEECH RECOGNITION

Upon the auditory detection of a designated wake word, such as "Hey Alice" or "Alice," the assistant initiates a sophisticated audio capture protocol, meticulously recording the subsequent vocal inputs. This listening state is underpinned by a robust acoustic processing framework that captures, segments, and streams the audio data to the Google Speech Recognition API, a cloud-based service renowned for its accuracy and efficiency in speech-to-text conversion. Once the speech is transcribed, Alice processes the text through its sophisticated algorithms, which are designed to understand and execute the user's commands. This process involves analyzing the text to determine the intent behind the words and then engaging the appropriate modules or actions within the system

SPEAKING OUT

Alice harnesses the advanced capabilities of Google's Text-to-Speech API to transform written text into lifelike, natural-sounding speech. This choice is pivotal for creating a more engaging user experience; while offline engines like PicoTTS and eSpeak offer functional speech synthesis, they often lack the fluidity and warmth of human speech, resulting in outputs that can seem mechanical and distant. In contrast, Google's solution is renowned for its ability to generate voice responses that closely mimic the intonation and rhythm of natural conversation. The API provides a rich selection of voices and languages, allowing Alice to deliver responses that are not only clear and accurate but also imbued with a human touch.
